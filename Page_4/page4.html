The Porsche 996

1998 - 2004

Units Produced: 175,262.

The Porsche 996, unveiled in 1998, marked a significant turning point in the storied history of the 911 series. As the first water-cooled engine variant in the lineup, it represented a bold departure from the air-cooled engines that had become synonymous with the 911's identity. This transition was met with mixed reactions, with some enthusiasts lamenting what they perceived as a loss of the car's inherent character.

In an effort to address some of the critiques and evolve the model's appeal, the 996 underwent a substantial facelift in 2001. This update aimed to improve its aesthetics, with an enhanced interior, and the introduction of a new Porsche Targa variant featuring a glass roof. However, this new Targa struggled to capture the same level of stylistic acclaim as its predecessors.

Despite the controversy surrounding its reception, the second generation of the 996 also brought the return of the 911 GT2. This iteration of the GT2 might have been somewhat tamer than its precursors from the 1990s, but it remained a formidable force. With a top speed of 196 mph and a turbocharged engine, yet lacking the assurance of a four-wheel-drive system, the mk2 996 GT2 maintained the series' reputation for offering a thrilling, high-performance driving experience.

The 996 era of the Porsche 911, while initially met with skepticism, ultimately contributed its own chapter to the evolving narrative of the brand, pushing technological boundaries and continuing the legacy of innovation and performance that has defined Porsche.



The Porsche 997

2004 - 2011

Units Produced: 213,004.

In 2004, Porsche rejuvenated the iconic 911 lineage with the introduction of the 997 generation, bringing back the cherished round headlights and refining the vehicle's design. While retaining the overall shape of its predecessor, the 996, the 997 boasted more intricate detailing and enhanced performance capabilities that were met with widespread acclaim.

Every model in the 997 series was equipped with engines delivering over 300bhp, signifying a significant step up in power and performance. The introduction of a new GT2 model pushed the boundaries even further, unleashing an unprecedented 530bhp, showcasing Porsche's relentless pursuit of automotive excellence.

The pinnacle of power was achieved with the introduction of the GT2 RS, which boasted an astonishing 611bhp, setting a new benchmark for the 911's performance. Meanwhile, the GT3 RS 4.0, also part of the 997 generation, was hailed by many as the best 911 ever produced. Its combination of power, precision, and driving dynamics encapsulated the essence of what makes the 911 series so revered among enthusiasts.

The 997 generation of the Porsche 911 not only honored the vehicle's rich heritage but also set a high standard for its successors, leaving an indelible mark on the history of this legendary sports car.
