The Original 911

1963 - 1973

Units Produced: 81,100.

In 1963, Porsche unveiled the 901 concept, a pivotal moment marking the beginning of what would become one of the most celebrated production runs in automotive history. However, a naming dispute with Peugeot led to the iconic rebranding of the 901 to the 911. Designed as a larger, more accommodating successor to the Porsche 356, the first 911 aimed to blend sportiness with practicality, introducing two extra seats and expanding its appeal.

Despite initial skepticism from critics regarding its focus, the early 911 models quickly silenced doubters. Powered by a two-litre engine delivering 130bhp (brake horsepower) and achieving a perfect balance with its one-tonne weight, the 911 demonstrated remarkable performance. Over the years, both the engine size and power output increased, leading to the creation of the highly revered Porsche 2.7 Carrera RS with 210bhp, a model that remains a pinnacle of early 911 iterations.

This first Porsche 911 set the stage for a legacy of innovation, design, and unparalleled driving experience, a legacy that continues to thrive and evolve, maintaining its esteemed position in the pantheon of automotive legends.



The G-Series

1973 - 1989

Units Produced: 196,397.

Introduced in 1973, the Porsche 911 G-Series marked a significant evolution in the iconic 911 lineage, representing not just a new iteration but the dawn of a new generation distinguished by noticeable differences in styling and technology. This series enjoyed an impressively long production run, lasting until 1989, and was pivotal in showcasing Porsche's commitment to innovation.

A landmark development during the G-Series era was the introduction of turbocharging to the 911 lineup. The arrival of the Turbo model in 1975, equipped with a formidable 260bhp engine powering the rear wheels, underscored Porsche's engineering prowess. At the time, turbocharging was a feature reserved exclusively for the pinnacle of performance models. The G-Series not only embraced this advanced technology but also set new standards for sports car performance, cementing the 911's legacy as a symbol of automotive excellence.
